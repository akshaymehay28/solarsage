# Module Cleaning: Methods and Economics

## Deciding when to clean

Clean when the value of recovered energy exceeds the cost of cleaning.
Measure soiling loss directly - a soiling station comparing a cleaned
reference device against a naturally soiled one, or analytics comparing
post-rain performance recovery - rather than cleaning on a fixed calendar.
Typical triggers are sustained losses of 3-5%. In rainy temperate climates
natural cleaning often suffices; desert sites may justify monthly cycles;
sites near quarries, agriculture (harvest dust, pollen), or bird colonies
need local schedules.

## Methods

Manual brushing with deionised water and soft brushes is the baseline:
effective, gentle if done correctly, labour-intensive. Water-fed pole
systems suit rooftops. Tractor-mounted rotating brush rigs clean utility
rows quickly but require compatible row geometry and careful pressure
control. Robotic cleaners - both rail-mounted units that live on the racking
and semi-autonomous crawlers - dominate large desert plants; dry-brush
robots avoid water logistics entirely at some cost in cleaning quality on
cemented dust.

Never use abrasives, high-pressure jets near frame seals, hot water on cold
glass, or untreated hard water, which leaves mineral films. Clean in early
morning or evening: cool glass, low output, and reduced thermal-shock and
glare risk for workers.

## Special soiling

Bird droppings cause concentrated hot spots and do not wash off in rain -
spot-clean them. Lichen and biofilm at module edges need mechanical removal.
Cement and pollen can cake into layers rain will not shift, which is why
post-rain recovery analysis distinguishes soiling types.
