# Module Cracking, Delamination and Encapsulant Failures

## Cell cracks and microcracks

Cell microcracks are caused by mechanical stress during transport, rough
handling during installation, hail, and heavy snow loads. They are invisible
to the naked eye but visible in electroluminescence imaging. A crack's impact
depends on whether it electrically isolates part of the cell: isolated
regions stop contributing current and the cell mismatch creates local
heating. Cracks grow over time under thermal cycling, so a module that tests
fine after installation can degrade years later.

## Snail trails

Snail trails are dark discolouration lines that follow the path of
microcracks, caused by silver migration into the encapsulant along moisture
that entered through the crack. They are a visible symptom of pre-existing
cracking, not an independent failure - modules with snail trails should be
EL-imaged and their power measured.

## Delamination

Delamination is the separation of the encapsulant (typically EVA) from the
glass or the cell surface, appearing as milky or bubbled regions. It admits
moisture, accelerating corrosion of cell metallisation and increasing the
risk of ground faults. Delamination near the module edge or junction box is
the most serious because it creates a direct moisture path. Affected modules
should be monitored and replaced when power loss or safety degradation is
confirmed.

## Encapsulant browning

Long-term UV exposure browns early-generation EVA, uniformly reducing light
transmission by several percent. Browning is gradual, affects whole module
batches equally, and appears in data as slow fleet-wide degradation rather
than a step change - distinguishable from soiling because cleaning does not
recover it.
