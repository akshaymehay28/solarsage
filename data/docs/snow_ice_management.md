# Snow and Ice on PV Arrays

## Losses and behaviour

Snow losses vary enormously by climate: from under 2% annual energy in mild
regions to 10-15% in heavy-snow regions with low-tilt arrays. Light snow
often slides off glass within hours of sun; the damaging pattern is a thaw-
freeze cycle that anchors an ice layer, or partial coverage where the lower
module rows stay buried after the top clears - which behaves like severe
partial shading, complete with bypass-diode heating.

Monitoring shows snow as near-zero output despite measured irradiance
(the irradiance sensor clears before, or after, the array does - a common
source of misleading performance ratios in winter).

## Mechanical risk

Wet snow is heavy: accumulations can exceed module static load ratings,
and sliding snow concentrates force on the lower frame edge and on anything
below the array. Cracked cells from snow load appear later as microcracks
in EL images rather than immediate failures. Check module load ratings
against local snow-load codes at design time; steeper tilt sheds snow
dramatically better.

## Response

In most climates the economic answer is to let snow melt - manual clearing
risks scratching glass and voiding warranties, and roof work in ice is a
safety hazard. Where clearing is justified (commercial roofs with high
winter tariffs), use soft roof rakes designed for PV and never metal tools
or hot water on cold glass (thermal shock). Bifacial and frameless modules
shed snow faster; module-level electronics reduce partial-coverage losses.
