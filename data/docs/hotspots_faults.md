# Hot Spots and Bypass Diode Faults

A hot spot occurs when a cell or group of cells dissipates power as heat
instead of generating it, usually because the cell is shaded, cracked, or
mismatched while the rest of the string continues to drive current through it.

## What hot spots indicate

Persistent hot spots visible in thermal (infrared) imaging indicate localised
cell damage, cracked cells, delamination, or failed solder joints. A single
cell running more than 10-15 degrees Celsius above its neighbours warrants
inspection. Left untreated, hot spots accelerate encapsulant browning and can
progress to burn marks, glass breakage, and in extreme cases fire risk.

## Bypass diode failure

Bypass diodes protect shaded cells by routing current around affected
substrings. A failed-short diode permanently removes one third of a module's
output — visible in IR imaging as one substring uniformly warmer than the
other two, and as a characteristic step in the module IV curve. A failed-open
diode removes the protection entirely, allowing severe hot spots under
partial shading. Diode failures are detected by IV curve tracing, IR
inspection under load, or string-level monitoring showing a fixed fractional
power loss.
