# Connector and Wiring Faults

Connector faults are among the most common physical defects found in field
inspections, and cross-mated connectors from different manufacturers are a
leading root cause even though they appear to fit together.

## Failure modes

A high-resistance connection - from a bad crimp, corrosion, or partial
mating - dissipates power as heat in a very small volume. Infrared inspection
shows a hot connector clearly against its neighbours; a temperature more than
10-20 degrees Celsius above comparable connectors warrants immediate
replacement, as the failure spiral (heat degrades contact, worse contact
makes more heat) ends in melted housings or series arcs.

Cable faults include insulation chafing on racking edges, UV embrittlement of
cable ties leading to cables sagging onto hot roof surfaces, water wicking
along conductor strands into junction boxes, and rodent chewing (a major
cause in some regions).

## Voltage drop and mismatch

Poor connections also cost energy without failing outright: added series
resistance shifts the string's IV curve, reducing fill factor. In string
monitoring this appears as one string consistently delivering slightly lower
current at the same voltage.

## Prevention

Use connectors of a single manufacturer per mating pair; torque junction box
terminations to specification; support cables so they cannot abrade or sit in
standing water; and include a sample thermographic scan of connectors in every
preventive maintenance round. Replace any connector that has been unmated
under load, as arcing damage inside may not be visible.
